# Universal Basic Income: A Radical, Materialist Case

> "For a colonised people the most essential value, because it is the most concrete, is first and foremost the land: the land which will bring them bread and, above all, dignity."
> — Frantz Fanon, The Wretched of the Earth (1961)

## What Universal Basic Income Is — and Is Not

Universal Basic Income (UBI) is a simple idea: every person receives a regular, unconditional payment from public funds. No means test. No work requirement. No humiliation. Its purpose is to guarantee a baseline of dignity and freedom from destitution.

But simplicity hides a fight over meaning. UBI can be emancipatory — or it can be a technocratic patch on a broken system. When financed and controlled by capital, it becomes a subsidy for low wages and privatised survival. When rooted in public power, it becomes a redistributive tool that shifts value, ownership and time back to people.

This essay takes the second path. It argues for a UBI grounded in **eco-social planning**, **public provision**, and **decolonial finance** — a basic income that weakens extractivism, not one that stabilises it.

---

Universal Basic Income looks simple on paper. Give everyone a cash floor. Remove conditionality. Reduce humiliation. Increase dignity.

Politics makes it complicated. How UBI is financed, governed and paired with public provision decides whether it is emancipation or management. Too often the conversation stops at the slogan. It favours technocratic fixes that preserve the power of capital.

UBI is a useful tool. It is not a cure. Alone, it will be co-opted. Only when embedded in an ecosocial programme — public services, democratic control of resources, decolonial finance — will it break the power structures that produce poverty and ecological collapse.

---

## Why This Debate Matters

Poverty and precarious labour are not natural states. They are manufactured through deliberate policy choices — austerity programmes, trade liberalisation, debt-adjustment schemes — that extract value from labour and land to feed capital accumulation. Wages are compressed, commons are enclosed, and public services are dismantled. A policy that treats poverty as individual failure will only manage symptoms whilst deepening the conditions that produce mass dispossession.

The UBI debate matters because it crystallises a fundamental political choice. Either we continue managing capitalism's human wreckage with targeted transfers and market-friendly interventions, or we use social policy to shift ownership, power and production towards collective control.

UBI has the potential to shift bargaining power between employer and worker. It can reduce the coercion that traps people in dangerous or underpaid work. It can free time for care, for organising, for political life. It can provide the security that enables people to refuse exploitation and build alternative economies.

But potential is not destiny. If the social wage is paid whilst structures of extraction remain intact, the money will be swallowed by profit, rent and debt. Landlords will capture housing subsidies. Healthcare companies will capture health spending. Financial institutions will capture debt relief. That is how reforms become stabilisers for the status quo — they provide just enough relief to prevent revolt whilst preserving the relations that produce suffering.

The question is not whether to support UBI, but which UBI to fight for, and as part of what broader programme of transformation.

---

## What Evidence Actually Shows

Evidence from pilots is useful, not miraculous. But it does reveal patterns that support structural analysis over technocratic optimism.

Large cash transfers reduce poverty and improve wellbeing. Trials across Kenya, Finland, Stockton (California), and elsewhere show improvements in food security, mental health and economic resilience. Recipients spend money on necessities, not luxury consumption. The lazy worker myth collapses under scrutiny.

Employment effects reveal the deeper political question. In contexts with strong labour protections and public services, UBI increases worker bargaining power without mass job abandonment. Where labour is precarious and services are privatised, cash helps survival but cannot address structural coercion.

The inflation question exposes class interests. Modest transfers to the poor produce minimal inflationary pressure. Fear of inflation typically masks concern that workers might gain leverage over wages and working conditions.

Trials also show a political truth: context and scale matter. Small, token payments change little. Universality, adequacy and complementary services change outcomes. The empirical bottom line is straightforward — cash helps. How far it transforms social relations depends on who controls the programme and what broader changes accompany it.

## The Predictable Failures of a Standalone UBI

When UBI is divorced from structural change, three failure modes always reappear. These are not hypothetical—they emerge from the logic of capital accumulation itself.

Marketising basic needs becomes inevitable when cash replaces public services rather than accompanying them. The poor pay more for health, housing and care. Private actors capture value and convert a social right into a private transaction. The result is commodified survival, not dignity.

A modest UBI can become justification for normalising cheap labour. Employers and governments argue that the social floor lets them compress wages. Without guarantees of labour rights, collective bargaining and public jobs, a basic income accelerates informalisation and precarious work.

Finally, ignoring ecological limits means increasing disposable income without reconfiguring production and supply chains. This increases demand that drives extraction. The so-called green transition is already being paid for by lands and bodies in the Global South. A UBI financed through regressive measures or by rents captured by foreign companies reroutes benefits to capital and deepens ecological harm.

**Here lies the central contradiction of market-friendly UBI: it promises individual security within a system that requires collective insecurity to function.** Capital needs a reserve army of unemployed workers to discipline wages. It needs cheap raw materials and energy to maintain profit rates. A universal income that increases consumption without changing production relations will intensify resource extraction and ecological breakdown.

These are not speculative outcomes. They form the playbook of neoliberal stabilisation: manage unrest with transfers, privatise commons, protect corporate profit. The social forces that benefit from this arrangement are clear — financial institutions managing poverty programmes, technology companies automating labour, extractive industries requiring social licence, and employers seeking wage subsidies. Against them stand workers, small farmers, indigenous communities, and all those whose labour and land are being enclosed for capital accumulation.

## What an Ecosocial UBI Must Look Like

**Democratic Foundations.**
If basic income is to be emancipatory, it must be part of a programme that changes ownership, governance, and flows of value.

**Universality and Adequacy.**
A universal, adequate floor must be established by democratic decision so it meets basic needs. Universality matters: means-testing creates stigma and bureaucratic control. Adequacy matters: a token sum will entrench precarity.

**Public Provision.**
Cash must sit beside layered public services — universal healthcare, childcare, education, public housing, and affordable transport. These decommodify life and prevent cash from being devoured by market rents.

**Financing Justice.**
Fund UBI through progressive wealth taxes, financial-transaction levies, windfall taxes on extractive profits, and taxes on pollution and land rents. Do not balance budgets on the backs of the poor. Cancel odious debts imposed on governments that financed extraction and predatory regimes. Debt cancellation is not generosity; it is rectification for centuries of theft.

**Reclaiming Resource Rents.**
Tax extraction and channel rents into the social wage and ecological restoration. Ensure revenues are transparent and accountable to communities rather than to foreign shareholders.

**Work and Dignity.**
Pair UBI with a jobs guarantee in care, ecosystem restoration, and decarbonisation. This rebuts claims that UBI undermines work — it broadens choice and strengthens labour’s bargaining power.

**Democratic Control.**
Resource rents and service delivery must fall under locally elected bodies, unions, and community councils. The Global South must reject models that outsource control to private capital or conditional lenders.

**Ecological Transformation.**
Ecosocial UBI avoids the consumption trap by pairing income support with production limits, planned obsolescence bans, and public ownership of key sectors. Instead of more individual consumption driving extraction, collective provision meets needs through efficient, democratically controlled systems. Public transport replaces private cars. Universal healthcare replaces private insurance. Community kitchens and shared resources replace individual consumption. The result is higher living standards with lower ecological impact.

**Global Solidarity.**
Wealth flight and corporate avoidance are transnational. Progressive tax cooperation, binding corporate accountability, and reparative transfers are essential to stop capital flight and fund just transitions. Global South countries implementing UBI whilst remaining locked into extractive export economies will see benefits flow to creditors and multinational corporations. Breaking this requires coordinated debt cancellation, fair trade terms, and technology transfers that enable diversified, locally controlled economies.

**Concrete Models.**
Elements of ecosocial UBI already exist. Brazil's Bolsa Família combined cash transfers with public healthcare, education, and nutrition programmes. South Africa's social grants, despite their limitations, demonstrate the political feasibility of large-scale redistribution. India's public food distribution system shows how universal provision can operate at massive scale. The task is integrating these elements within a coherent programme of democratic economic planning.

**Mobilisation and Clarity.**
These are practical demands, not technocratic fantasies. They require mobilisation, conviction, and moral clarity.

## Answering the Usual Objections

_"It will make people lazy."_
Empirical evidence rejects this. Cash increases security and dignity. It creates time for care and organising. It does not produce mass idleness.

_"It's unaffordable."_
Affordability is a political choice. Military budgets, tax giveaways for capital and debt servicing are choices. Money exists where power allows it. Tax the wealth that hoards income. Close tax havens. Cancel illegitimate debts.

_"It will undermine organising."_
A secure floor enlarges political space. It gives people time to organise, to strike, to build institutions. That is why capital fears it. A small, poorly designed transfer might pacify; a universal, adequate and institutionally embedded income empowers.

---

## Building the Political Coalition

The path from here to ecosocial UBI runs through organised political struggle, not policy advocacy. It requires building a coalition that can challenge capital's control over social reproduction and ecological systems.

The potential coalition includes formal and informal workers whose labour is devalued and insecure. It includes communities resisting extractive industries and land grabbing. It includes women whose care work is unpaid and undervalued. It includes young people facing ecological collapse and economic exclusion. It includes indigenous peoples defending land rights and traditional economies.

This coalition must organise around immediate demands that build towards structural transformation. Living wage campaigns that raise worker bargaining power. Public ownership campaigns that take key services out of private hands. Debt justice campaigns that break the chains of financial extraction. Climate justice campaigns that link ecological and economic transformation.

The strategy is not to wait for perfect conditions but to use each struggle to build the organisational capacity and political consciousness necessary for deeper change. Ecosocial UBI becomes both a mobilising demand and a concrete expression of an alternative economic model.

---

## A Final, Sharp Demand

UBI can be liberation if we make it so. It will not be by sliding cash into the market and hoping for the best.

Insist on an income that is universal and adequate. Fund it from wealth and extraction, not from cuts to the poor. Pair it with universal public services and a public jobs guarantee. Place control in democratically accountable hands. Build international mechanisms that prevent capital flight and deliver reparative finance.

This is not compassion. It is justice. It is a redistribution of what was stolen.

Fight for a basic income that is a foundation for collective life, not a bandage for empire.

— In solidarity with workers, community organisers and ecological defenders in the Global South and beyond.
